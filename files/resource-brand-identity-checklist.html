Section 1 — 12 Points

Logo & Visual Identity

Your logo is the single most recognizable element of your brand. It appears on everything from your website favicon to a billboard. A strong logo system works at every size, in every context, and communicates professionalism before a single word is read.

✓
Logo works at 16px (favicon size) without losing recognizability
Test by shrinking your logo to 16x16 pixels. If you can't identify it, you need a simplified favicon version.
✓
Logo is available in full-color, single-color black, and single-color white versions
You need these for dark backgrounds, light backgrounds, single-color printing, and embroidery.
✓
Vector file formats exist (SVG, AI, or EPS) — not just PNG or JPEG
Vector files scale infinitely without quality loss. If you only have raster files, your logo will look blurry on large formats.
✓
Clear space rules are defined (minimum breathing room around the logo)
Most brand guides define clear space as the height of one letter in the logo. This prevents visual crowding.
✓
Minimum size rules are documented (smallest acceptable reproduction size)
Below a certain size, fine details disappear. Define the smallest width your logo can be reproduced at.
✓
Logo has both horizontal (full) and stacked (compact) layout options
Different placements require different orientations. A square social profile needs a stacked version.
✓
A standalone icon or mark exists that can represent the brand without the wordmark
Think of how Nike uses just the swoosh. Your brand needs a recognizable symbol for small spaces and app icons.
✓
Logo is not dependent on color to be understood (works in grayscale)
Print a grayscale version. If the logo loses meaning or becomes unreadable, it relies too heavily on color.
✓
"Do not" examples are documented (stretching, recoloring, rotating, adding effects)
Showing incorrect usage is just as important as showing correct usage, especially when multiple people handle the brand.
✓
Logo passes the "squint test" — recognizable when blurred or seen from a distance
Step back 10 feet from your screen. If the logo becomes a shapeless blob, it lacks strong enough form.
✓
Logo files are organized in a central, accessible folder with clear naming conventions
Every team member should know exactly where to find the right logo file. Use names like "logo-full-color.svg" and "logo-white.png".
✓
Logo has been tested on all common backgrounds (white, black, photography, patterns)
Place your logo on product photos, your website header, and a dark background. Does it maintain clarity and contrast in each?
⚡ Quick Win
Create a single shared folder (Google Drive or Dropbox) with subfolders for each logo version: /full-color, /white, /black, /icon-only. Name each file with its use case. This 15-minute task eliminates 90% of "wrong logo" mistakes.
✓ Good Practice
Organized brand asset folder with SVG, PNG, and favicon versions
Clear space of at least 1x logo height on all sides
Separate icon mark for social profiles and app icons
✗ Common Mistake
Single JPEG logo file emailed around the team
Logo crammed into corners with no breathing room
Full wordmark logo squeezed into a circular profile picture

Section 2 — 8 Points

Color Palette & Typography

Color is processed by the brain 60,000 times faster than text. Your palette and type choices create an emotional response before anyone reads a word. Consistency here builds instant recognition — think of how quickly you recognize a Coca-Cola red or a Tiffany blue.

📈 Brand Consistency Stat
80%Brand recognition increases by up to 80% when consistent colors are used across all touchpoints.
✓
Primary color palette limited to 3–5 colors with defined hex codes
Too many colors dilute recognition. Define your exact hex, RGB, and CMYK values for each color.
✓
Secondary/accent colors defined for UI elements, highlights, and callouts
Secondary colors support the primary palette for buttons, links, alerts, and promotional materials.
✓
Color accessibility checked — sufficient contrast ratios for text on backgrounds (WCAG AA minimum)
Use a tool like WebAIM Contrast Checker. Body text needs a 4.5:1 ratio; large text needs 3:1 minimum.
✓
Color usage rules defined (which color is used for what — headings, backgrounds, CTAs, etc.)
Without usage rules, your blue might appear on buttons on one page and as a background on another, creating inconsistency.
✓
Primary heading typeface selected and documented with specific weights
Choose 1–2 weights for headings (e.g., Regular and Bold). Document the exact font name, weight, and fallback stack.
✓
Body text typeface selected with defined sizes for web, mobile, and print
Minimum 16px for web body text, 14px for mobile. Define your type scale: body, small, caption, and heading sizes.
✓
Font licensing verified for all intended uses (web, desktop, app, commercial print)
Many fonts have separate licenses for web embedding, app bundling, and commercial print. Verify you are covered for all use cases.
✓
Typographic hierarchy established (H1 through H4, body, caption, label styles)
Each level should have a defined size, weight, line-height, and letter-spacing. This creates visual order and scannability.
⚡ Quick Win
Create a one-page color and type reference card with your hex codes, font names, and sizes. Pin it in your team Slack or print it and stick it next to your desk. This single sheet prevents 80% of "what color/font is that?" questions.
✓ Good Practice
Primary: #1D1D1F, Accent: #4ABD98, with defined tints at 10%, 20%, 40%
Playfair Display for headings, Inter for body — documented with weights
✗ Common Mistake
"Our color is kind of a teal-green" with no documented hex code
Using 4+ different fonts across website, social, and print

Section 3 — 9 Points

Brand Voice & Messaging

Your brand voice is how you sound in every piece of communication — from a tweet to a customer service email. Without documented voice guidelines, every person who writes for your brand creates a slightly different personality, confusing your audience about who you actually are.

✓
Brand voice documented with 3 adjectives (e.g., "confident, approachable, witty")
Three adjectives create a clear, memorable framework that anyone writing for the brand can reference quickly.
✓
3 "we never" rules defined (e.g., "we never use jargon," "we never talk down to customers")
Boundaries are just as important as direction. "We never" rules prevent off-brand communication faster than positive guidelines alone.
✓
Elevator pitch exists — who you are, what you do, and why it matters in under 30 seconds
Every employee should be able to describe the business the same way. Write it down, practice it, and make sure the team knows it.
✓
Tagline or slogan is concise, memorable, and communicates your unique value
A good tagline is 3–7 words, focuses on customer benefit, and passes the "could a competitor say this?" test. If yes, it's too generic.
✓
Key messaging pillars defined (3–5 core themes your content consistently reinforces)
Messaging pillars ensure every blog post, social caption, and email supports a strategic narrative — not random topics.
✓
Tone variations documented for different contexts (social media, formal emails, support tickets)
Your voice stays the same but your tone shifts. Playful on Instagram, professional in proposals, empathetic in support — all still "you."
✓
Unique value proposition (UVP) clearly stated and different from competitors
Your UVP answers "why choose you over alternatives?" If your answer could apply to any competitor, it is not a real differentiator.
✓
Customer-facing copy uses "you" language (customer-centric, not company-centric)
Compare "We offer great service" with "You get dedicated support." The second speaks to the customer, not about yourself.
✓
Approved boilerplate descriptions exist for bios, press releases, and directory listings
Write 25-word, 50-word, and 100-word versions. These get copy-pasted constantly — make sure they are polished and consistent.
⚡ Quick Win
Write your 3 voice adjectives and 3 "we never" rules on a sticky note and put it on your monitor. This 5-minute exercise immediately improves the consistency of every email, post, and page you write.
✓ Good Practice
Voice: "Confident, approachable, expert" — documented with examples for each
"We never use buzzwords or scare tactics to sell"
Three boilerplate lengths: 25, 50, and 100 words
✗ Common Mistake
"Our voice is professional" with no further definition
Website says "innovative solutions," social says "hey fam" — no consistency
Every directory listing describes the business differently

Section 4 — 10 Points

Digital Presence Consistency

Your digital presence spans your website, social profiles, email signatures, Google Business listing, and more. Inconsistency across these touchpoints signals disorganization to potential customers — and it costs you trust before you ever get a chance to earn their business.

✓
Website reflects current brand colors, fonts, and logo (not an outdated version)
Audit your website against your brand guidelines. Old logos and previous color schemes are surprisingly common.
✓
All social media profiles use the same logo version, bio format, and link structure
Open Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, and TikTok side by side. The profile photo, bio, and link should be visually and verbally consistent.
✓
Google Business Profile is claimed, verified, and matches website branding
Your Google listing is often the first impression. Make sure hours, photos, description, and logo match your brand identity.
✓
Email signatures are standardized across the team with logo, colors, and consistent formatting
Every email is a brand touchpoint. Create a signature template that the entire team uses — not just the founder.
✓
Favicon is set on the website and matches the brand icon
That tiny icon in browser tabs is seen every single day. A missing or generic favicon is an instant credibility loss.
✓
Open Graph images are set for social media sharing (custom branded preview when links are shared)
When someone shares your website link on social, does a branded preview appear? Or is it a random image or blank? Set og:image tags.
✓
404 page is branded and helpful (not the default server error page)
A custom 404 page with your branding, navigation, and a search bar turns a frustration into a brand moment.
✓
Social media post templates exist for recurring content types (quotes, tips, promotions)
Pre-designed templates ensure every post looks on-brand, even when created under time pressure.
✓
Email marketing templates match website branding (header, footer, button styles, colors)
If your website is clean and modern but your emails look like 2010 templates, you are breaking the brand experience.
✓
Online directory listings (Yelp, Apple Maps, industry directories) show consistent name, logo, and description
Search for your business name. Every listing should show the same name spelling, logo, and description. Inconsistency confuses both customers and search engines.
⚡ Quick Win
Do a "brand safari" right now: open every platform where your brand appears (website, Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, Google, email signature) in separate tabs. Compare them side by side. You will likely find at least 2–3 inconsistencies you can fix today.
⚠ Common Pitfall
Updating your logo or colors on your website but forgetting to update social profiles, email signatures, and directory listings. Create a checklist of every place your brand appears and update them all at once during any rebrand.

Section 5 — 8 Points

Print & Physical Materials

Physical materials create tangible brand moments that digital cannot replicate. A business card, packaging, or signage is something a customer holds, touches, and keeps. Inconsistency between your digital and physical brand creates cognitive dissonance that erodes trust.

✓
Business cards match the current brand identity (logo, colors, fonts, not an old version)
If your business cards look different from your website, you are handing out confusion. Reprint after any brand update.
✓
CMYK color values defined for print production (not just RGB/hex)
Screen colors (RGB) and print colors (CMYK) are different systems. Without defined CMYK values, your printer will approximate — and it will look wrong.
✓
Letterhead and document templates exist with brand header, footer, and formatting
Proposals, invoices, and letters should all carry your brand. A Word or Google Docs template takes 30 minutes to create and lasts years.
✓
Packaging design (if applicable) aligns with digital brand identity
Unboxing is a brand experience. If your packaging looks like a different company than your website, you are wasting a powerful touchpoint.
✓
Signage and storefront branding matches the current brand identity
Your physical sign may be the first thing a customer sees. Make sure it reflects the same brand they will find online.
✓
Branded merchandise or uniforms (if used) follow the brand color palette and logo guidelines
Employee t-shirts, hats, or aprons are walking advertisements. Make sure the logo placement and colors are correct.
✓
Print-specific versions of the logo exist for single-color printing (foil stamping, embossing, engraving)
Special print techniques require simplified, single-color logo files. Have these ready before you need them.
✓
Presentation templates (PowerPoint/Keynote/Google Slides) are branded and available to the team
Sales decks and investor pitches should look as polished as your website. A branded template ensures consistency across every presentation.
⚡ Quick Win
Create a branded Google Slides or PowerPoint template with your logo in the corner, brand colors as the palette, and your heading/body fonts preset. Share it with your team. This single template elevates every pitch, proposal, and internal presentation.
✓ Good Practice
Print color guide with Pantone, CMYK, and RGB swatches side by side
Branded proposal template that matches the website design
Business cards, letterhead, and signage all aligned to same guidelines
✗ Common Mistake
Handing the printer your logo as a low-res JPEG and hoping for the best
Team members creating their own slide decks with random fonts and colors
Storefront sign from 3 years ago with the old logo still hanging
